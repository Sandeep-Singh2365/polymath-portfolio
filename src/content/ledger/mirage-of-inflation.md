In Chapter 22 of *Economics in One Lesson*, Henry Hazlitt outlines a core thesis: **inflation is a form of hidden taxation.** 

When a government builds a massive highway, bridge, or public park, the public is awed by the tangible results. However, they rarely ask the crucial systems question: *how was this paid for?*

### The Deficit Illusion
If a public project is paid for out of the proceeds of direct taxation, the transaction is clear. Wealth is transferred from private citizens (who now have less to spend on private goods) to the state (which builds the public work). The net purchasing power of the economy remains unchanged; only its distribution shifts.

But what if the state funds these projects through **deficit financing**—borrowing or directly resorting to the central bank's "printing press"? 

At first glance, the public works appear to be created out of thin air. Jobs are created, concrete is poured, and money circulates. This is the **Mirage of Inflation**.

```
[Deficit Financing] ──> [Supply of Currency Increases] ──> [Value of Currency Decreases] ──> [Prices Rise]
```

### The System Mechanics of Price Dilution
Printing money does not create wealth; it dilutes it. 
1.  **Supply & Demand:** By increasing the quantity of money while the quantity of real goods remains constant, the value of each individual unit of currency drops.
2.  **Lagged Price Rise (The Cantillon Effect):** The new money does not reach everyone at the same time. The government and its immediate contractors receive the printed currency *first*, purchasing goods at pre-inflation prices. By the time the money trickles down to the general worker, prices of food, rent, and commodities have already risen. 
3.  **The Hidden Tax:** The difference between the initial value of the money and its subsequent diluted purchasing power is the "inflation tax" paid by the middle and lower classes.

### The Programmer's Analogy: Floating Point Precision
In software, trying to represent infinite real values using a fixed-bit floating-point variable leads to rounding errors and a loss of precision. 

Similarly, in economics, attempting to represent infinite public spending by printing a variable currency against a finite basket of national goods leads to price precision errors (inflation). You cannot optimize a system's output by simply shifting the decimal point on its unit of measure.
