Part III of the Constitution of India guarantees Fundamental Rights to its citizens. However, most of these rights are enforceable only against **"The State."** 

This constraint makes **Article 12** one of the most critical legal interfaces in Indian jurisprudence. It defines the exact boundaries of who can be sued for violating a citizen's basic freedoms.

### The Constitutional Definition
According to Article 12, the term 'State' includes:
1.  **The Executive & Legislative organs of the Union:** The Government of India and the Parliament.
2.  **The Executive & Legislative organs of the States:** State Governments and their Legislatures.
3.  **All Local Authorities:** Municipalities, Panchayats, District Boards, and Port Trusts.
4.  **Other Authorities:** The ultimate judicial battleground.

### The Judicial Battleground of "Other Authorities"
The phrase "Other Authorities" is a linguistic catch-all. Over decades, the Supreme Court of India had to determine if public corporations, autonomous societies, and private bodies performing public functions fall under this definition.

Through landmark cases like *Rajasthan SEB v. Mohan Lal* and *RD Shetty v. International Airport Authority*, the courts established the **"Instrumentality of State"** test. If a body meets the following criteria, it is classified as the State:
*   **Financial Control:** The state holds the entire share capital or major funding.
*   **Deep and Pervasive Control:** The state regulates its daily administrative operations.
*   **Public Duty:** The functions performed by the body are of deep public importance and closely related to governmental duties.
*   **Monopoly Status:** The body possesses state-conferred monopoly power.

```
[Financial Funding] + [Pervasive Admin Control] + [Public Function] ──> Instrumentality ──> Enforceable Fundamental Rights
```

### The System Analyst's Perspective
In object-oriented programming, we use the `interface` to define a contract that subclasses must implement. Article 12 is the constitutional interface for accountability. 

By applying the "Instrumentality" test, the judiciary prevents the government from escaping its Part III obligations. If the government could simply wrap its core functions (like energy, transit, or housing) in independent corporations and claim they are "private entities," the constitutional contract would be broken. 

Article 12 ensures that no matter how the state wraps its logic (encapsulation), its core public interfaces remain bound by fundamental rights.
